Catholic Holy Week

Palm Sunday to Easter Sunday

The most sacred week in the Catholic liturgical year — commemorating the Passion, Death, and Resurrection of Jesus Christ.

St. Anna's Sunday School • 6th & 7th Grade

Holy Week at a Glance

Holy Week (Hebdomada Sancta) begins on Palm Sunday and ends on Easter Sunday. The Sacred Paschal Triduum — the holiest days of the entire Church year — begins with the evening Mass of the Lord's Supper on Holy Thursday, reaches its high point in the Easter Vigil, and closes with Evening Prayer (Vespers) on Easter Sunday.

Palm Sunday

Palm Sunday of the Passion of the Lord

Jesus enters Jerusalem in triumph — the crowds shout "Hosanna!"

🌿

Palm Sunday — What Happened

The Triumphal Entry

Jesus entered Jerusalem riding on a donkey. Crowds spread palm branches and cloaks on the road, shouting praises. This fulfilled the Old Testament prophecy of the Messiah entering Jerusalem humbly.

"Hosanna to the Son of David; blessed is he who comes in the name of the Lord; hosanna in the highest."

— Matthew 21:9 (NABRE)

"Exult greatly, O daughter Zion! Shout for joy, O daughter Jerusalem! Behold: your king is coming to you, a just savior is he, humble, and riding on a donkey, on a colt, the foal of a donkey."

— Zechariah 9:9 (NABRE)

"So they took branches of palm trees and went out to meet him, and cried out: 'Hosanna! Blessed is he who comes in the name of the Lord, the king of Israel.'"

— John 12:13 (NABRE)
🌿

Palm Sunday — Traditions

🫒

Blessing & Procession of Palms

Mass begins with a special procession. Palms are blessed by the priest and distributed to the faithful, who carry them into the church.

📖

Reading of the Passion

The full Passion narrative is read aloud during Mass, from one of the Synoptic Gospels (Matthew, Mark, or Luke) on a three-year cycle.

✝️

Palms Are Kept

Catholics take blessed palms home and display them (often behind a crucifix). These palms are later burned to create ashes for the next Ash Wednesday.

🔥

Did You Know?

The liturgical celebration of Jesus' entry into Jerusalem has been observed since at least the 4th century. The pilgrim Egeria recorded palm processions in her travel diary (c. 381–384 AD).

Historical Importance

Palm Sunday marks the beginning of Holy Week. Red vestments are worn because they symbolize the Passion and martyrdom — pointing ahead to the sacrifice of the Cross.

🏛️

Monday of Holy Week

Cleansing of the Temple

Jesus drives out the money changers — "My house shall be a house of prayer."

🏛️

Monday — What Happened

Cleansing of the Temple

Jesus returned to Jerusalem. According to the Gospels, He cleansed the Temple by driving out the money changers and merchants. This demonstrated His authority and zeal for proper worship of God.

It also increased the hostility of the chief priests and scribes, who began seeking a way to destroy Him (Mk 11:18).

The Anointing at Bethany

The Gospel reading for Monday of Holy Week (Jn 12:1–11) recounts Mary of Bethany anointing Jesus’ feet with costly perfumed oil. Jesus said, “Leave her alone. Let her keep this for the day of my burial” (Jn 12:7) — foreshadowing His coming death.

"Jesus entered the temple area and drove out all those engaged in selling and buying there. He overturned the tables of the money changers and the seats of those who were selling doves. And he said to them, 'It is written: My house shall be a house of prayer, but you are making it a den of thieves.'"

— Matthew 21:12–13 (NABRE)

"For my house shall be called a house of prayer for all peoples."

— Isaiah 56:7 (NABRE)
📖

Tuesday of Holy Week

Teaching in the Temple

Jesus teaches the Greatest Commandment — love God and love your neighbor.

📖

Tuesday — What Happened

Jesus Teaches

Jesus spent much of this day teaching in the Temple. He debated with the Pharisees and Sadducees, told parables, and gave His discourse about the end times (the Olivet Discourse).

Catholics are encouraged to go to Confession (the Sacrament of Reconciliation) during Holy Week in preparation for Easter.

"You shall love the Lord, your God, with all your heart, with all your soul, and with all your mind. This is the greatest and the first commandment. The second is like it: You shall love your neighbor as yourself. The whole law and the prophets depend on these two commandments."

— Matthew 22:37–40 (NABRE)

"‘Amen, I say to you, whatever you did for one of these least brothers of mine, you did for me.’"

— Matthew 25:40 (NABRE)

Jesus also gave the parable of the judgment of the nations — the sheep and the goats — teaching that how we treat the least among us is how we treat Him (Matthew 25:31–46).

🪙

Spy Wednesday

The Betrayal of Judas

Judas Iscariot agrees to hand Jesus over for thirty pieces of silver.

🪙

Spy Wednesday — What Happened

Judas' Betrayal

Judas Iscariot went to the chief priests and agreed to betray Jesus for thirty pieces of silver. This act of betrayal set the Passion in motion.

This day is called "Spy Wednesday" because Judas acted as a spy against Jesus.

The Chrism Mass

During Holy Week (usually on Holy Thursday morning, though some dioceses celebrate it earlier in the week), the bishop blesses the Sacred Chrism, the Oil of Catechumens, and the Oil of the Sick — the three holy oils used in the sacraments throughout the year. Priests renew their ordination promises.

"Then one of the Twelve, who was called Judas Iscariot, went to the chief priests and said, 'What are you willing to give me if I hand him over to you?' They paid him thirty pieces of silver, and from that time on he looked for an opportunity to hand him over."

— Matthew 26:14–16 (NABRE)

"And they weighed out my wages, thirty pieces of silver."

— Zechariah 11:12–13 (NABRE)
🍞

Holy Thursday

Thursday of the Lord's Supper

Jesus institutes the Eucharist, washes His disciples' feet, and prays in the Garden.

🍞

Holy Thursday — What Happened

The Last Supper

Jesus celebrated the Last Supper with His apostles — the Passover meal at which He instituted the Holy Eucharist and the Sacrament of Holy Orders (the priesthood).

He washed the feet of His disciples as an act of humble service. After supper, He went to the Garden of Gethsemane, where He prayed in agony and was arrested.

"This is my body, which will be given for you; do this in memory of me… This cup is the new covenant in my blood, which will be shed for you."

— Luke 22:19–20 (NABRE)

"If I, therefore, the master and teacher, have washed your feet, you ought to wash one another's feet. I have given you a model to follow, so that as I have done for you, you should also do."

— John 13:14–15 (NABRE)

"I give you a new commandment: love one another. As I have loved you, so you also should love one another."

— John 13:34 (NABRE)

"My Father, if it is possible, let this cup pass from me; yet, not as I will, but as you will."

— Matthew 26:39 (NABRE)
🍞

Holy Thursday — Traditions

🫗

Washing of the Feet (Mandatum)

The priest washes the feet of members of the congregation, imitating Christ's act of humble service to His apostles.

🕯️

Altar of Repose

After Mass, the Eucharist is carried in procession to an Altar of Repose. The faithful keep watch in adoration — as Jesus asked His apostles in Gethsemane.

⛪

Stripping of the Altar

The altar is stripped bare and the church is left unadorned to symbolize Christ's abandonment and humiliation.

🔔

Bells Are Silenced

Church bells are silenced after the Gloria and do not ring again until the Easter Vigil — a sign of mourning.

Historical Importance

Holy Thursday commemorates the institution of two sacraments: the Eucharist and Holy Orders. Jesus commanded, "Do this in memory of me" (Lk 22:19), establishing the priesthood and the sacrifice of the Mass.

✝️

Good Friday

Friday of the Passion of the Lord

Jesus is crucified, dies, and is buried. He gave His life freely for the salvation of all humanity.

✝️

Good Friday — What Happened

The Crucifixion

Jesus was tried before Pontius Pilate, scourged, crowned with thorns, and forced to carry His cross to Calvary (Golgotha). He was crucified, died, and was buried.

Jesus gave His life freely for the salvation of all humanity.

His Blessed Mother, Mary, stood at the foot of the Cross (Jn 19:25). From the Cross, Jesus entrusted Mary to the beloved disciple and the disciple to her — a moment the Catholic Church sees as Jesus giving Mary as mother to the whole Church.

"It is finished."

— John 19:30 (NABRE)

"But he was pierced for our sins, crushed for our iniquity. He bore the punishment that makes us whole, by his wounds we were healed."

— Isaiah 53:5 (NABRE)

"Father, forgive them, they know not what they do."

— Luke 23:34 (NABRE)

"When Jesus saw his mother and the disciple there whom he loved, he said to his mother, 'Woman, behold, your son.' Then he said to the disciple, 'Behold, your mother.' And from that hour the disciple took her into his home."

— John 19:26–27 (NABRE)
✝️

Good Friday — Traditions

🚫

No Mass Is Celebrated

Good Friday is the only day of the year (along with Holy Saturday) when Mass is not celebrated. Instead, a solemn Celebration of the Passion takes place, usually at 3:00 PM.

✝️

Veneration of the Cross

A large cross is presented and the faithful come forward to reverence it by a kiss, genuflection, or bow. This practice dates to at least the 4th century in Jerusalem.

🚶

Stations of the Cross

A devotion following Jesus' journey from His condemnation to His burial in 14 stations. Many parishes pray this together.

🍽️

Fasting & Abstinence

Obligatory fasting (one full meal, two smaller meals) and abstinence from meat for Catholics ages 18–59 (fasting) and 14+ (abstinence).

Historical Importance

Good Friday is the most solemn day in the Catholic calendar. Catholics believe that through His death, Jesus redeemed humanity from sin and opened the gates of heaven. The Good Friday liturgy has three parts: Liturgy of the Word, Veneration of the Cross, and Holy Communion.

🕯️

Holy Saturday

The Great Sabbath

The Church waits in silence. Jesus descends to the dead to bring salvation to the righteous.

🕯️

Holy Saturday — What Happened

In the Tomb

Jesus' body lay in the tomb. The Church waits in silence and prayer. Catholic tradition holds that Christ descended to the dead (the "Harrowing of Hell") to bring salvation to the righteous who had died before Him.

The Apostles' Creed states: "He descended into hell" — meaning the realm of the dead, not the hell of the damned.

"For Christ also suffered for sins once, the righteous for the sake of the unrighteous, that he might lead you to God. Put to death in the flesh, he was brought to life in the spirit. In it he also went to preach to the spirits in prison."

— 1 Peter 3:18–19 (NABRE)

"Taking the body, Joseph wrapped it in clean linen and laid it in his new tomb that he had hewn in the rock. Then he rolled a huge stone across the entrance to the tomb and departed."

— Matthew 27:59–60 (NABRE)
🔥

The Easter Vigil — The Most Solemn Liturgy

🔥

1. Service of Light

A large Easter fire is blessed outside the darkened church. A brand-new Paschal Candle — prepared fresh each year — is inscribed with a cross, the current year, and the Greek letters Alpha and Omega, signifying that Christ is the beginning and end of all things. The priest inserts five grains of incense into the candle, representing the five wounds of Christ. The new Paschal Candle is then lit from the Easter fire, symbolizing the Risen Christ as the Light of the World. As the candle is carried into the dark church, the faithful light their own candles from its flame, and the joyful hymn known as the Exsultet (Easter Proclamation) is sung.

📖

2. Liturgy of the Word

Up to seven Old Testament readings trace salvation history — from Creation (Gen 1) through the Exodus (Ex 14) to the prophets — followed by the Epistle and Gospel.

💧

3. Liturgy of Baptism

Catechumens receive Baptism, Confirmation, and First Eucharist. The baptismal water is blessed. All the faithful renew their baptismal promises.

🔔

4. Liturgy of the Eucharist

The first Mass of Easter is celebrated. Church bells ring again during the Gloria! The church is decorated with flowers and light.

Historical Importance

The Easter Vigil is the oldest and most solemn liturgy in the Church, with roots to the earliest centuries of Christianity. Baptizing new Christians at the Easter Vigil dates to at least the 2nd century. "If, then, we have died with Christ, we believe that we shall also live with him." — Romans 6:8

Easter Sunday

The Resurrection of the Lord

He is not here, for He has been raised just as He said!

🌅

Easter Sunday — What Happened

The Resurrection

Jesus Christ rose from the dead. Early on Sunday morning, women went to the tomb and found it empty. An angel announced that Jesus had risen. He appeared to His disciples alive.

The Resurrection is the foundation of the entire Christian faith.

"Do not be afraid! I know that you are seeking Jesus the crucified. He is not here, for he has been raised just as he said."

— Matthew 28:5–6 (NABRE)

"Jesus said to her, 'Mary!' She turned and said to him in Hebrew, 'Rabbouni,' which means Teacher."

— John 20:16 (NABRE)

"And if Christ has not been raised, then empty too is our preaching; empty, too, your faith."

— 1 Corinthians 15:14 (NABRE)

"We know that Christ, raised from the dead, dies no more; death no longer has power over him."

— Romans 6:9 (NABRE)
🌅

Easter Sunday — Traditions

⛪

Easter Sunday Mass

Celebrated with great joy. White and gold vestments are worn. The church is decorated with lilies. The Alleluia, silent during Lent, returns!

🗣️

The Easter Greeting

"Christ is risen!" — "He is truly risen!" Catholics around the world greet each other with these words of joy.

📅

Eastertide — 50 Days

Easter is not just one day — it begins the Easter Season, lasting 50 days until Pentecost Sunday. Every Sunday is a "little Easter."

🎵

Victimae Paschali Laudes

The ancient Sequence hymn "Praises to the Paschal Victim" is sung at Mass. The Paschal Candle remains lit at every liturgy.

Historical Importance

Easter is the oldest and greatest feast in the Catholic Church. The entire liturgical year revolves around it. The celebration of Easter on a Sunday is the reason Catholics gather for Mass every Sunday — each Sunday is a celebration of the Resurrection.

Key Catholic Terms

Paschal Triduum

The three holiest days — beginning with the evening Mass of the Lord's Supper on Holy Thursday, through Good Friday and Holy Saturday, and concluding with Evening Prayer on Easter Sunday — celebrating Christ's Passion, Death, and Resurrection.

Eucharist

The Body and Blood of Christ, instituted by Jesus at the Last Supper.

Passion

The suffering and death of Jesus on the Cross.

Liturgy

The official public worship of the Church, especially the Mass.

Paschal Candle

A large candle prepared new each year, inscribed with a cross, the year, and Alpha/Omega. It is lit from the Easter fire at the Easter Vigil and symbolizes the Risen Christ as the Light of the World. It remains lit throughout the Easter Season and at Baptisms and funerals.

Veneration of the Cross

The act of showing reverence to the Cross on Good Friday.

Stations of the Cross

A 14-station devotion following Jesus' path from condemnation to burial.

Chrism

Sacred oil blessed by the bishop, used in Baptism, Confirmation, and Holy Orders.

Catechumen

A person preparing to be baptized and received into the Catholic Church.

Alleluia

A joyful exclamation meaning "Praise the Lord," omitted during Lent and restored at Easter.
